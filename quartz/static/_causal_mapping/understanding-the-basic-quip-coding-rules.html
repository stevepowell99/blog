Causal Mapping

Section 24 Understanding the basic QuIP coding rules

James and Fiona, I think I’ve been rushing too much in trying to fit QuIP causal diagrams into one or other preconceived schema. In this note, I want to try to understand and characterise QuIP coding rules as they stand, comparing and contrasting with other possibilities.

I’d like to ask if I’ve managed to characterise QuIP correctly, and if not, to correct me!

Actually I will focus on QuIP methods as presented in BSDR (2017), e.g. before any innovations like the coding of multiple influence items or positive versus negative links – partly to make really sure that any suggested innovations still retain all the added value of the tried-and-tested QuIP method.

I will also address only the basic coding rule (atomic causal maps and causal chains), and only touch on issues surrounding synthesis and aggregation.

It is not so hard for me to make the causal map app offer whatever BSDR wants it to offer. The question is only how. Some features of the QuIP coding process differ from the most generic, SEM-like way to construct causal maps, but most of these can be easily recast, behind the scenes, in terms of a more generic approach, with little or no loss of fidelity. But I need to make sure which “QuIP quirks” are important and need preserving in exactly their current form, and which can just be adapted. For example, in the original approach causal chains are always linear with no possibility of multiple influence items. Maybe this is an arbitrary limitation or maybe there is/was a good reason for it: if a single causal chain can have multiple influence items, doesn’t it become harder to judge if the encoded claim is attributable to the project?

How does QuIP encode (and possibly synthesise) multiple fragments of causal information?

There are many approaches which use directed graphs to encode causal information, but differ in the details. QuIP as I understand it takes a particularly interesting (and deliberately restrictive, relatively conservative) stance.

QuIP coding is explicitly graphical in the formal sense, because the units of coding are causal chains of items, along with a restricted set of additional features (e.g. “attribution”). It would be possible to express the result of a QuIP coding exercise as a graph even if that had never been the intention. Some of the most important QuIP syntheses are however not graphical, but take the form of tables derived by querying databases of causal chains and their additional features. The main aim of QuIP is not to produce a perfect Theory of Change for a particular context, but to provide summary presentations of plausible linkage from projects to positive changes in outcomes of interest, which themselves are only an invitation to explore the (narrative) detail of the individual causal links.

Terminology: I will follow the terminology in the Glossary in BSDR (2017), together with our recent additional Glossary (also, I will call the nodes of the causal maps “items”, which is a more neutral and appropriate term than “variables”).

I have argued that a good way to understand different approaches to causal mapping is to look at the different rules they use, implicitly or explicitly, for encoding and combining causal fragments. I will use this method here. My main aim is to elucidate the QuIP stance in terms of its version of these rules.

24.1 Classic QuIP: overview

The QuIP protocol begins by asking questions about changes in pre-defined domains of interest like nutrition over a reference period. Sources mention items important for them in that domain and work backwards from each to provide chains of antecedent causes.

The focus of, and main motivation for, QuIP is the extent to which interventions of interest are mentioned spontaneously as causal antecedents to the key outcomes. See later.

24.1.2 The nature of the items

24.1.2.1 The items are (subjective) propositions, not variables

I believe that QuIP tends to treat the items (the boxes or nodes between which arrows are drawn) as propositions and not, for example, as variables. The items themselves (and not just the links) are specific empirical (but not causal) claims made in and about a specific context (such as “X has increased” or “Y has got worse”) and are, broadly speaking, more or less true or false in that context. (The claims have a varying amount of subjectivity, from a fairly subjective “this has got worse for me” to a fairly intersubjective “the rains have got worse”; nevertheless they are grammatically all claims, which can in principle be more or less true or false.)

Presumably, in the example above, we could if necessary be more precise in writing “presence of unemployment” rather than just “unemployment”, to make it clear that this is something that can be true or false, rather than just the name of something.

(Whereas, if the items are understood were to be understood as variables, as in SEM, e.g. amount of hunger, level of unemployment, these are not things which can be true or false; specification of the actual / current / predicted value of the variables is an additional, optional step.)

24.1.2.2 The items have to have been individually experienced

Also, the propositions have to be / have been personally true for / experienced by the source.

From a SEM perspective, this seems bizarre. But from the perspective of, say, an evaluator conducting an ordinary summative evaluation of a project, it is a completely normal way of thinking. This kind of statement usually carries a lot more weight for the reader than hypothetical statements.

(The above also implies that all the propositions are in fact, perhaps even necessarily, in the past.)

24.1.2.3 Do the items necessarily express changes? States? Events?

Can we be more precise? Perhaps each item has to express a change, e.g. from the beginning to the end of a reference period? But this doesn’t work here in the case of “unemployment” which is mentioned as a reason why for having less money, and perhaps remained at a high level throughout the reference period. Perhaps the source even explicitly mentioned “if we’d had jobs, we could just have worked overtime to compensate”, contrasting unemployment only with its absence, namely employment. Here, unemployment is not mentioned as something which changes over time; rather, it is contrasted with another situation which might have existed outside the reference period or simply as a desirable but non-existent situation.

The definitions in the Glossary in BSDR (2017) are not completely consistent. So, a Driver is “An action or state (X or Z) behind outcomes (Y).” and Outcomes are “Changes (positive or negative) reported by respondents”; as “outcomes can also become drivers of change”, this means that the apparent distinction between “actions or state” on the one hand and “changes” on the other hand is blurred. My understanding is that actually, it is final (“no-child”) outcomes which have to be expressed as changes in the reference period, whereas other items can be expressed in a variety of (propositional) ways.

Putative causes like “unemployment” are, I believe, a frequent headache in QuIP coding: inflation, migration, God’s will … things whose status may not be changing, and which may have a role in the language-game of “explanation” in a particular social context but may not really help with understanding causes. (Of course, causal claims about changes in an influencing factor like “increasing unemployment” are also possible and not so problematic.)

A strength of this kind of proposition-based analysis of causal statements is that it quite naturally captures the kinds of causal explanations which people actually give. One could argue that we shouldn’t even be worrying about what format the antecedent causes take, because they are just whatever explanations people give. But this is problematic, because the coder needs to understand the items within a claim, rather than just reproducing them verbatim, in order to be able to judge whether a different claim by a different source involves the same item or not.

24.1.2.3.1 Events

There seems no reason why events shouldn’t be causes too.

In this case, the event can also be parsed as a change, from lower to higher food taxes. But …

… in this case, the triggering event can hardly plausibly be described as a change. It is a clear example of a one-off event.

“Events” pass the contrastive test (see below): if the incursion hadn’t happened, we wouldn’t have lost (so many) cattle, but it did, and we did.

(Actually, events like this are harder to capture with a generic SEM approach in which the items are variables.)

24.1.2.3.2 Do final outcomes have to be changes? status quo

We haven’t got very far: it seems the items might be changes, or events, or states … we have only suggested above that, according to the QuIP protocol, at least the final outcomes have to be expressed as changes. And indeed, if that is the way the very questions are formulated, we have to accept it. It would mean we can’t use QuIP to evaluate projects which aim to maintain the status quo, and that it is difficult to use QuIP to evaluate any project with a moving baseline. This seems wrong. In the above case, the very point of the project is to maintain the status quo.

24.1.2.3.3 Do final outcomes have to be changes? one-off events

The commissioner of this evaluation might have been involved in, say, providing the preparation and is very interested in improving the retention of first-time workers over the reference period. But the individual students interviewed as part of this evaluation are not, from their own individual perspectives, describing changes of any kind; they are describing an experience which is by definition a one-off. They are describing contrasts with hypothetical alternatives, not with prior states experienced by the respondents themselves. “I really knew what to expect and the people knew what support to give, much better than what my cousin said it is like where she lives.”

Weddings, births, transition to first employment etc, and their specific characteristics are all stand-out examples of events for which are obviously important in individual life cycles and are often mentioned in QuIP interviews but which, at least from the perspective of individuals, can only with difficulty be characterised as changes over a reference period.

24.1.2.3.4 Conclusion on events / states / changes

I think there is nothing for it but to replace the idea of “changes” with the broader idea of “differences” or “deltas” (https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/dear-world-bank-its-differences-changes-we-look-impact-steve-powell/).

All the examples above can be covered by the idea of “differences”; things being one way rather than some expected or possible contrast: a state of unemployment rather than a state of employment, a status quo in flood levels rather than the feared contrast of floods, an incursion of terrorists rather than none, and so on. This would also allow QuIP to evaluate projects with the aim of maintaining the status quo, although obviously the questions would have to be formulated a little differently.

My question is, if indeed deltas or differences are not an acceptable alternative or more general characterisation of the nature of the items in QuIP causal maps, rather than considering them to be changes / states / events, do we have a more systematic account of their nature?

24.1.3 The nature of the links

24.1.3.1 Contrastive implications

I think that the QuIP way of coding a causal claim concurs with the mainstream that it always implies a “counterfactual” contrast (which is quite another thing from saying that the people making it have to be in possession of corresponding evidence). If someone makes a claim of the form that “X is a causal package containing the main factors which causally influenced Y”, but then say that actually even if X had not happened in that way, this wouldn’t have made any difference to Y, and that Y can happen or not happen irrespective of X", I think that a QuIP coder too would conclude that the speaker does not share our understanding of causality, and would not code the initial statement as a causal claim.

For example, if someone says that the reason my crops were good is because it is the Year of the Dolphin, and the reason my neighbour’s crops were poor is also because it is the Year of the Dolphin, if they have not named further explanatory factors we would probably not code this/these as causal claims.

24.1.3.2 Only “plus” links?

I believe there is an ?implicit? rule that each mini-map represents an overall “plus” influence on the consequence item. Otherwise, it wouldn’t make sense to say in summary that “there were 21 causal chains leading to positive change in the health domain which were explicitly attributed to the project” - because if “minus” links were allowed, the project might have been serving to suppress or reverse those positive changes.

Said differently, if a revised QuIP is going to allow minus as well as plus links, this simple way to summarise project impact has to be revisited.

24.1.3.3 Mono versus multiple causation

In “classic QuIP”, there is only one influencing item for each consequence item in a causal chain.

Allowing causal packages with more than one influencing item means revisiting the simple “atomic map” rule above. This might not be as simple as it sounds, not least because of the provision “items have to have been individually experienced”.

In this case, there is a simple, continuous outcome. The project has a small, plus effect on it, which is outweighed by another larger, minus influence.

The logic of “attribution” coding is tricky here. On the surface, this is a negative change, a negative outcome, which is also (partially) attributable to the project! This would make the project seem bad, whereas in fact the specific contribution of the project is positive.

Also the requirement that “the items and causal mechanisms have to have been actually experienced by the respondent” is harder to understand/implement here. It’s not quite clear what this means in the case of two opposing, if independent, forces. If I watch two ropes pulling on an object with equal force in opposite directions, do I really experience the causal effects of the two opposing forces in the same way that I might experience just one rope pulling unopposed on the same object?

Even in these very simple, additive cases, as soon as we introduce multiple influence items, the very grass-roots, narrative / propositional approach taken by QuIP is more difficult to apply.

24.1.3.4 Explicit causal knowledge

I think it is also important that what is to be coded is an explicitly causal claim. It is not enough that the respondent says “I noted that X changed. I also noticed that Y changed.” The QuIP protocol is designed to draw on respondents’ underlying causal world-view, and not primarily on observation of co-occurrence.

24.1.3.5 Causal chains

QuIP has the additional notion of a causal chain of two or more such causal links. But I think that, also for QuIP, the notion of causality expressed with this kind of atomic link is the same regardless of the position it might take within a causal chain. In other words, we don’t need different “atomic coding rules” depending on whether the link in question is at the beginning, middle or end of a chain. A coder means the same thing when coding such a link regardless of its position, and someone reading the map understands the same thing.

That is good: the simpler and more universal the rules, the better.

24.1.3.6 Causal chains and attribution

On the other hand, the concept of an entire causal chain is still important in QuIP and, on the face of it, not reducible to a bunch of individual links. This is because attribution codes are given to whole chains, not to individual links.

It’s useful that BSDR has now adapted the coding process to be built up from individual atomic maps, rather than chains. The question is to what extent attribution codes are irreducible or can now potentially be “read off” from the rest of the causal map. If we know an outcome is positive, and it has causal antecedents which are clearly (products of) the project, down a convincing causal path, don’t we already know the attribution status without having to code it specially? What if we restrict ourselves to interpreting causal chains (from project to outcome) which were explicitly mentioned by individual respondents rather than pieced together from multiple sources.

If not, what else is missing?

24.2 Problems

24.2.1 Contrasts

A weakness is that the contrast situations are not always clear. So we can reasonably ask: if “unemployment” exerts some kind of causal influence here, it can’t be eternally true or present, presumably there are situations or contexts would count as a lack of unemployment, but which? Full employment? 3% unemployment? 30% unemployment?4

24.2.2 Focus on initial items

One problem with the notion of drivers as the beginning of a causal chain is that it obscures the question to what extent intermediate links are not merely determined by the driver (plus noise) but are independently responsible for the final changes. In this case, unemployment could be seen as a driver in its own right and/or as subject to other unnamed influences.

24.2.3 Ability to make evaluative statements

But it is really problematic to make any evaluative statement about this, for example, that this may have been a substantial or significant contribution.

It is our responsibility as researchers/evaluators in the real world to not be too conservative, to tend too much to committing type II errors rather than type I errors.

24.2.4 Vulnerability to luck / coincidence

So if Brazil and Germany play in the World Cup Final, and Brazil wins narrowly, there will be endless post-hoc discussion about the “failures” in the German side and all the good decisions / strategy / tactics on the Brazil side, even though everyone knows it could just have easily gone the other way: over-interpretation of happenstance. As a practical evaluation technique, QuIP does this. For example, if a perfectly-conceived and prepared project is denied success by an incredibly unlucky meteorite strike at the last minute, QuIP is unable even to begin an investigation, because there are, unluckily, no “positive changes” to put at the end of causal chains. QuIP is not alone in this: most narrative approaches to evaluation would have the same problem. Whereas a broader social-science perspective might ask to what extent the project was the right kind of device to address the problem, whether or not it was denied success in an unforseeable way at the last moment.

This is the difference between evaluating overall success potential and actual success / failure.

There is a big tension between plausibility and validity.

On the other hand, the Ambassador might well have trouble understanding why that kind of evaluation might give a project full marks even though it was ultimately unsuccessful.

(The same problem works the other way round, when a poor project “achieves” positive changes via lucky pathways.)

24.3 Conclusion

So perhaps we can say:

A QuIP causal graph is a DG consisting of propositions including one or more key items of interest are expressed as a change over the reference period, together with their causal antecedents, and antecedents of those antecedents, and so on. Also, the propositions have to be / have been personally true for the source.

Also: any graph consisting of nodes connected by arrows is a directed graph (DG). (Directed Acyclic Graphs or DAGs are just DGs in which cycles are forbidden. For this note, the question of whether the graph may include any loops or cycles is not very important.)

24.3.1 Synthesis

The Yin-Yang article does explicitly talk about synthesis, also in the context of decodification.

Post-hoc recoding of items into a smaller set of more comprehensive items. This too is a form of synthesis.

24.3.1.1 Evaluating the causal contribution of the intervention to the outcomes of interest

“attribution. How many casual claims have we identified that link each outcome area back to causes or drivers of change that explicitly refer to the project, are implicitly consistent with the project’s theory of change, or completely incidental to it?” BSDR (2017)

It is easy to deduce on the basis of a QuIP diagram if there were any mentions of the intervention having a causal contribution.
